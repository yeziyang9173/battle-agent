From a user-behavior angle, the first-minute risks are usually the most obvious affordances, not the “intended flow.” Ordered by likelihood, I’d smoke test these 5 interaction patterns first: **1) immediate movement/exploration** (click, tap, drag, WASD, swipe), **2) clicking the most visually salient object** (door, button, glowing item, avatar, hotspot), **3) repeated input when feedback feels delayed** (double-clicking, spam tapping, key mashing), **4) camera/viewport changes** (resize, rotate, zoom, mobile orientation shift), and **5) leave-and-return behavior** (back, refresh, reconnect, reopen room). Those are the highest-probability actions and the fastest way to expose broken state, weak feedback, or input conflicts.

The highest-risk pattern in practice is often **input ambiguity**: users won’t ask “what mode am I in?”—they’ll just try movement, object interaction, and camera control interchangeably. If the room has any rich affordance at all, they’ll also test boundaries fast: “Can I open this, move this, or trigger this?” So for smoke, I’d treat **first response latency**, **visible confirmation**, and **state persistence after interruption** as the key pass/fail metrics across all 5 patterns.
